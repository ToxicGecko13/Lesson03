How is a knife forged?


WANING: Forging is very dangerous! You should not attempt it without extensive training and an expert’s supervision!


Picking Your Steel

You first want to begin with picking the right steel. There are many kinds, and you want to do extensive research to find out what one is best for you. Here are a few examples of different steels, S30V is superior at holding an edge, but it’s hard to sharpen, 420HC holds an okay edge, but it’s easier to sharpen, and 420J2 is nice to work with at the forge. There are MANY more, that’s just a few. So do your research on what steel will work best for what you want it to do.


Designing Your Knife

There are tons of different types of knives. You can do one of the many specific types, a few of which are a bowie, machete, or dagger. You could also mix a few different types of knives, or design your own. But whatever you do, make a blueprint of your knife, and make sure the drawing is the same size as what you want your knife to be.


Forging Your Knife

Once you have the steel and blueprint for your knife, you should be able to start forging. A couple of the important things to remember are, to not overheat the billet (a piece of metal normally in a rectangular shape that you forge into something) burnt steel is extremely brittle, and never hammer on cold steel, it can create cracks and micro fractures, which can intern result in catastrophic failures, like your blade braking in half well in use.


Heat Treatment and Tempering

A heat treat is what hardens the steel, it can be done many different ways, one of which being, heating the knife up intel it’s not magnetic anymore, and then quickly cooling it done by dunking it in oil (quenching) or one of many different substances used for quenching. This proses hardens the steel but that also makes it more brittle, and the type of steel can diced how hard and brittle it becomes, so again depending on what you are planning to use it for, you can temper it to be less hard and intern less brittle. Tempering is usually done by heating it to 175–350°C (347–662°F) for 2 hours, which normally results in a good balance between sharpness retention, how easy it is to sharpen, and toughness. So again the recipe for tempering will depend on what you’re using the knife for.


Making Your Handle

Picking the right handle material can be important, if it’s more of a pretty knife rather than a tough knife, then you probably want some kind of synthetic material like micarta or G10 rather than a natural material like wood or leather, but those can be very nice looking too, if there done right. After you decide on a handle material, then you can start designing it, this is very important for it to be comfortable, appealing, functional. Comfortable and functional should always come first though. It can include stuff like a guard, finger hold, pommel, and lanyard hole. So design your ideal handle, make sure it fits your needs, and then make it a reality. Take your time and perfect it to fit your hand like a custom made glove.


Rustic or Pretty

This can be the last step, decide if you want it to be really pretty, like a mirror finish on the steel, or if you want it a little rustic, like leaving some hammer marks is the steel. After you decide that, ether clean it up, or leave it a little messy, and then you’re finished!



AGAIN, do NOT attempt forging without extensive training and an expert’s supervision! And these are pretty much the absolute basics, so reading this does not make you an expert!
